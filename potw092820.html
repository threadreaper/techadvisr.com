The Best Web Browser You're Not Using

Brave is private, fast, and open source. What's not to love?

September 28, 2020
Michael Podrybau Senior Technical Lead

You browse the web nearly every single day, probably using the same web browser you've been using for years. They're all the same anyway, right? Modern web browsers are designed to deliver a reliable and consistent user experience, regardless of which one you choose or what device you use it from. But there's something different about the new kid in town, and you need to learn why.

It's familiar.   The new Brave browser is built on the open-source Chromium codebase just like Google Chrome. According to netmarketshare.com Chrome currently has a stranglehold on the browser market, accounting for 69.19% of all web browsing, so there's a good chance you're already familiar with it. At first glance, Brave looks like any other Chrome clone. The interface and menus are nearly the same, and you can even use the same URL shortcuts used by Chrome to access various information and browser settings, such as chrome://version and chrome://settings. Brave also supports the full range of Chrome extensions and can migrate bookmarks and settings from ANY browser installed on your system. On the small chance that you're NOT a Chrome user, you'll still feel almost instantly at home in Brave. However, Brave is anything but a copycat.

It's FAST   The team behind Brave provides plenty of info on their site to support this claim. But benchmarking environments are not always a very accurate representation of everyday browser usage. Fortunately, Brave performs even better in the real world than it does on paper. The user experience is polished and smooth, without any of the quirks we've come to expect from "new" software. Brave renders even the heaviest of pages at breakneck speed (3.8 seconds to load Hulu.com versus Chrome's 10.7, for example). And if you're one of the many people who usually have about 75 more tabs open than you need, you know it can lead to sluggish browsing performance. Most web browsers keep all of your open tabs fully loaded in memory. But Brave attaches the currently focused tab to its main worker thread on the CPU and unloads most of the resources assigned to background tabs. The result is a consistently smooth experience even with dozens of tabs open. And Brave is smart enough to recognize when you're playing music or other media from a backgrounded tab and won't interrupt your listening.

It's private.   Brave provides a built-in ad-blocker and privacy minder known as Brave Shields. There's a little shield icon on the address bar to toggle Shields. (It's on by default) Brave Shields blocks intrusive ads and thwarts all of the various trackers and spyware from companies like Google and Facebook who totally have your best interests in mind and aren't doing anything nefarious with your personal information... *ahem* Brave's ad-block and privacy protection features are the most aggressive built into any modern browser, which is a good thing, but there is a caveat. Brave can overreact and attempt to block things that can break the functionality of websites. If this happens, you can just toggle the "Shields down" for the affected page. Before you commit to this choice, Brave will show you how many trackers and ads it has already blocked on that site. If it's an extreme number, you may find it preferable to leave the page rather than expose your system and information to it.

Chrome is known for recording just about everything you use it for and happily phones that information home to Google's data centers. The Brave team claims their browser doesn't do anything of this sort. I'm happy to report that after some pretty rigorous analysis, their claim seems to hold up. If all of these privacy enhancements still aren't enough for you, Brave features built-in one-click access to the Tor network. If you're unfamiliar with Tor, it's a routing protocol designed to prevent anyone who may be monitoring your connection from knowing what websites you visit. When used correctly, Tor does an excellent job of making it exceptionally difficult for anyone to associate you with your web traffic. But it can be kind of a pain to get set up. Brave makes routing your traffic through Tor just as easy as opening an "incognito" tab in any other browser.

In an age where all browsers are starting to feel very same-same, Brave sets itself apart from the first moment you start using it. Modern browsers have become bloated with "features" that most users will never discover, let alone care about. Brave returns us to a simpler time, trimming the fat and delivering a refreshingly snappy and stable browsing experience. The additional features it delivers are easy to appreciate, even for non-technical users. Head to https://brave.com to download Brave for all your devices, absolutely free, and enjoy a faster, safer web.
